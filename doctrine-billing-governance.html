Doctrine

Billing Governance

Billing departments in FQHCs operate under conditions that standard billing management frameworks are not designed for. Billing governance is the operational infrastructure that makes FQHC billing consistent, auditable, and correctable without continuous external oversight.

The Problem: Execution Without Architecture

FQHC billing fails when the billing department is expected to execute correctly without the operational infrastructure to support consistent execution. When encounter rate rules are not documented as executable procedures — when payer-specific requirements are held in individual staff knowledge rather than written protocol, when denial response is reactive rather than structured, when billing performance has no regular internal review — the department is being asked to produce consistent results from an inconsistent system. The failures that follow are systemic, not personal.

What Billing Governance Covers

Billing governance covers the full operational cycle of a billing department: claim preparation standards aligned to FQHC encounter rate requirements; payer-specific submission rules written as executable procedures; denial classification and response protocols; payment posting standards; accounts receivable management procedures with defined follow-up logic by aging category; and an internal review schedule that identifies process drift before it compounds into revenue loss.

The Upstream Connection

Billing governance cannot be isolated from clinical documentation and intake. A billing department that executes its own procedures correctly but receives inadequately documented encounters will still produce denials. Governance that addresses only the billing department without addressing the upstream conditions producing the failures it receives is incomplete. GAKK designs billing governance as part of an integrated operational substrate, not as a standalone billing department intervention.

What GAKK Builds

We develop billing governance frameworks that the organization can operate without external dependency: procedure documentation aligned to the specific payer mix and contract terms; denial classification and response protocols; an internal billing audit schedule; and integration points with the documentation and intake governance systems that feed into billing. The deliverable is a system the billing department can run, not a set of recommendations for them to interpret.

If your billing department is producing inconsistent results despite competent staff, the problem is likely structural. We can assess it.
